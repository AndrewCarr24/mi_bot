# MI Regulatory Landscape

> Private mortgage insurers operate under a multi-layered regulatory framework that combines state insurance regulation with GSE/FHFA oversight through the Private Mortgage Insurer Eligibility Requirements (PMIERs). This dual structure was significantly strengthened after the 2008 financial crisis, shifting the industry from exposure-based statutory capital to risk-based capital standards. The FHFA, as conservator of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, drives counterparty standards for MIs, while state insurance regulators oversee solvency, contingency reserves, and market conduct. The result is one of the most tightly regulated sectors in housing finance, designed to ensure MIs can pay claims through severe economic stress.

## What it is

The regulatory framework governing private mortgage insurers operates at two primary levels. At the state level, MIs are regulated as insurance companies by state insurance departments, which set statutory capital requirements, contingency reserve rules (typically 50% of premiums held for ten years), and risk exposure limits — in most states, twenty-five dollars of risk in force for every dollar of regulatory capital (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). State regulation focuses on solvency and claims-paying ability.

At the federal level, the FHFA oversees MIs indirectly through its role as conservator of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. The GSEs' charter acts require credit enhancement on loans exceeding 80% LTV, and each Enterprise determines which MIs qualify as approved counterparties (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). PMIERs were significantly updated in 2015 and again in 2018 with the revised PMIERs 2.0, which addressed lessons learned from the housing downturn (INDUSTRY_USMI_RESILIENCY_2023-11); PMIERs 2.0 became effective March 31, 2019 for existing approved insurers (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_2.0_BASE). These requirements replaced the earlier reliance on rating agency approvals with specific risk-based capital standards, liquidity requirements, and operational benchmarks.

The PMIERs require an MI's Available Assets to exceed its Minimum Required Assets, defined as the greater of a risk-based stress scenario projection or a $400 million floor (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). The risk-based calculation uses a grid of factors including vintage, original LTV, credit score, and delinquency status to project claims under stress.

## Why it matters

The regulatory framework is the single most important determinant of MI capital adequacy, pricing discipline, and counterparty reliability. For analysts, the PMIERs sufficiency ratio — the ratio of Available Assets to Minimum Required Assets — is the key metric for assessing an MI's financial strength and its capacity to write new business. The industry has consistently maintained levels well above the 100% minimum; USMI members collectively held $11 billion in excess, representing a 169% sufficiency ratio as of late 2023 (INDUSTRY_USMI_RESILIENCY_2023-11).

The post-crisis regulatory enhancements transformed the MI business model. Prior to 2008, MIs operated under exposure-based statutory capital with less granular pricing and a "buy-and-hold" approach to risk. The PMIERs framework, combined with the Dodd-Frank Act's Ability-to-Repay/Qualified Mortgage requirements and improved GSE representation and warranty frameworks, created a more resilient system (INDUSTRY_USMI_RESILIENCY_2023-11). The shift to risk-based pricing, active portfolio management, and programmatic credit risk transfer (CRT) through reinsurance and insurance-linked notes has made MIs stronger counterparties.

The regulatory structure also creates a barrier to entry. Newly approved insurers must demonstrate initial capital of at least $500 million, obtain a rating within three years, and face dividend restrictions during their first three years (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). This limits the competitive landscape to well-capitalized players.

## Current state (as of 2024-12-31)

The regulatory environment in 2024 reflected continued FHFA focus on counterparty risk management and housing finance system resilience. The FHFA's 2024 Report to Congress highlighted several ongoing priorities: improving the Enterprises' capital positions under the Enterprise Regulatory Capital Framework (ERCF), enhancing appraisal data quality through the Uniform Appraisal Dataset redesign (targeting broad production in January 2026), and implementing updated credit score requirements (FICO 10T and VantageScore 4.0) (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

The Enterprises' CRT programs remained a core part of their risk management, transferring credit risk on approximately $365 billion in UPB in 2024, though volume declined for the second consecutive year due to lower mortgage acquisition volume (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). The FHFA also continued to explore opportunities to harmonize the GSEs' representation and warranty frameworks.

The draft revised PMIERs, which FHFA released for public comment, proposed additional requirements including documented risk diversification policies, claims processing timelines (180 days from claim perfection), and restrictions on providing contract underwriting services that create material contingent liabilities (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). These proposals reflect the FHFA's ongoing effort to ensure MIs can withstand high-risk loan concentrations without jeopardizing claims-paying ability on lower-risk portfolios.

## How it has evolved

The regulatory framework has undergone three distinct phases. **Pre-2008**: MIs were regulated primarily at the state level with exposure-based statutory capital limits (25:1 risk-to-capital). Each GSE maintained its own eligibility requirements, relying heavily on rating agency approvals rather than Enterprise-defined standards (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). The industry used published rate cards with broad LTV-based pricing and minimal CRT.

**Post-crisis reform (2008-2015)**: The financial crisis exposed weaknesses in the MI regulatory model. MIs and GSEs suffered significant losses from defaults and foreclosures. The Dodd-Frank Act (2010) established the FHFA as independent regulator and conservator. The FHFA directed the GSEs to develop aligned, risk-based counterparty standards, culminating in the first PMIERs in 2015.

**PMIERs era (2015-present)**: The 2015 PMIERs introduced risk-based capital requirements using stress scenario grids. The 2018 PMIERs 2.0 further refined the framework. Since then, MIs have raised over $2.8 billion in equity and debt, and the industry has transferred nearly $73.8 billion in risk through CRT transactions on over $3.4 trillion of insurance-in-force (INDUSTRY_USMI_RESILIENCY_2023-11). The business model evolved from "buy-and-hold" to "aggregate-manage-distribute," with MIs using quota share reinsurance, excess-of-loss transactions, and insurance-linked notes to distribute risk to capital markets.
