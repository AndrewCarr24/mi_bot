# The GSE Relationship (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, FHFA)

> Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac (the GSEs) are the primary customers of the US private mortgage insurance industry, purchasing or guaranteeing the vast majority of conventional mortgages that require MI. The Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) oversees both GSEs and, through the Private Mortgage Insurer Eligibility Requirements (PMIERs), sets the capital and operational standards that MIs must meet to be approved counterparties. This three-party structure — MIs, GSEs, and FHFA — defines the competitive landscape, capital requirements, and business volume for the six major mortgage insurers.

## What it is

The GSE relationship is the structural foundation of the private mortgage insurance industry. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac are government-sponsored enterprises chartered by Congress to provide liquidity and stability in the secondary mortgage market. Their charter acts require credit enhancement on any mortgage with a loan-to-value ratio exceeding 80%, and private mortgage insurance is the most commonly used form of that credit enhancement (INDUSTRY_FREDDIE_PMI_HANDBOOK_2021-09).

FHFA serves as the GSEs' conservator and regulator. Under FHFA's direction, the GSEs jointly developed and maintain the PMIERs, which establish the financial and operational requirements that MIs must satisfy to become and remain "Approved Insurers" (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). Each GSE publishes its own set of PMIERs, but the requirements are aligned under FHFA oversight.

The relationship is bilateral in practice: MIs provide first-loss protection on GSE-acquired loans, reducing the GSEs' loss severity in default scenarios, while the GSEs provide the MIs with their primary source of new business volume. The GSEs also have broad remedial authority under the PMIERs, including the ability to impose volume limits, require additional capital, suspend, or terminate an MI's approval status (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA).

## Why it matters

For MI analysts, the GSE relationship is the single most important external factor affecting industry profitability and risk. Key implications include:

- **Volume dependency**: The GSEs purchase or guarantee the majority of US conventional mortgages. MI industry new insurance written (NIW) is therefore heavily correlated with GSE acquisition volumes and the share of low-down-payment loans in that flow.
- **Capital regime**: PMIERs capital requirements, set by the GSEs under FHFA oversight, are the binding capital constraint for all six MIs. Changes to PMIERs directly affect how much capital MIs must hold against their risk-in-force, influencing return on equity and dividend capacity.
- **Counterparty risk**: The GSEs have unilateral authority to impose restrictions on MIs that fall out of compliance. PMIERs §901 enumerates 21 remediation actions; the consequential tiers include imposing new-business volume or risk limits, requiring additional capital infusion, restricting delegated underwriting, increasing the Minimum Required Assets, commuting or restructuring existing risk-in-force, differentially pricing loans based on the MI provider, and ultimately suspension or termination of approval status (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). This gives the GSEs substantial leverage over MI operations.
- **Pricing dynamics**: The GSEs can differentially price loans based on the MI provider, effectively creating a pricing signal that influences lender and borrower choice of MI carrier.

## Current state (as of 2025-06-13)

As of FHFA's 2024 Report to Congress (published June 2025), the key developments in the GSE-MI relationship include:

- **PMIERs update**: In August 2024, the Enterprises issued updates to the PMIERs, which outline financial and operational standards for private mortgage insurance companies (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). This was the most recent update to the eligibility requirements.
- **Conservatorship continues**: FHFA remains conservator of both GSEs. Director William J. Pulte (arrived March 2025) opens FHFA's FY2024 Report to Congress (published 2025) with a deregulatory, pro-growth posture: his foreword names "combating waste and fraud, ensuring compliance with all applicable laws, eliminating unnecessary regulations, and boosting productivity" as immediate priorities, frames the GSEs as "engines of America's housing revival," and explicitly declines to take positions on pre-tenure FHFA activity (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).
- **Credit score transition**: The GSEs are implementing updated credit score requirements (FICO 10T and VantageScore 4.0), which may affect the risk profile of loans flowing through the MI channel (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).
- **Coverage requirements unchanged**: Standard MI coverage requirements remain as outlined in Freddie Mac's PMI Handbook, with coverage ranging from 6% to 35% depending on LTV and loan term (INDUSTRY_FREDDIE_PMI_HANDBOOK_2021-09).

## How it has evolved

The GSE-MI relationship has undergone significant structural change since the 2008 financial crisis:

- **Pre-2008**: Each GSE maintained its own MI eligibility requirements, relying primarily on rating agency ratings rather than specific counterparty financial standards. Requirements had not been updated since 2003 (Fannie Mae) and 2008 (Freddie Mac) (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA).
- **Post-crisis reform**: The financial crisis revealed that many MIs lacked sufficient capital to withstand a severe stress scenario. FHFA, as conservator, directed the GSEs to develop aligned, updated PMIERs to ensure MIs maintain financial strength adequate to survive a severe macroeconomic downturn (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA).
- **PMIERs (2014-2015)** → **PMIERs 2.0 (effective March 31, 2019)**: The aligned PMIERs framework was first drafted by FHFA in 2014 (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA) and took effect in 2015, then refined as PMIERs 2.0 effective March 31, 2019 for existing approved insurers (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_2.0_BASE) — introducing the current risk-based capital model.
- **August 2024 update**: The most recent PMIERs revision refined operational requirements, quality control programs, and claims processing standards (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

The relationship has shifted from a relatively passive approval process (relying on rating agencies) to an active, quantitative counterparty risk management framework where the GSEs continuously monitor MI financial health and have broad intervention authority.
