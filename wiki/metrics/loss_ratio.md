# Loss Ratio (MI-specific)

> The loss ratio is the fundamental measure of underwriting profitability for U.S. private mortgage insurance, calculated as losses incurred (net of reinsurance) divided by net premiums earned. Unlike standard P&C insurance, MI loss ratios are frequently *negative* in benign credit environments because favorable reserve releases on prior-year delinquencies can exceed losses on new notices in a given year. Loss ratios moved decisively into positive territory across the six U.S. MIs in 2025 — for the first time in years, only Arch's mortgage segment is still printing a negative number — as the post-COVID reserve cushion winds down and the 2022-2024 origination vintages season into the natural loss-emergence period. Year-end 2025 loss ratios: Arch (mortgage segment) (0.4)%, MGIC 5.1%, Radian (Mortgage Insurance segment) 7.1%, NMI 9.6%, Enact 11%, Essent (Mortgage Insurance segment) 16.8% (ACGL_10-K_2025-12-31; MTG_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31; NMIH_10-K_2025-12-31; ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31).

## What it is

**Definition.** Per MGIC's glossary, the loss ratio is "the ratio, expressed as a percentage, of losses incurred, net to net premiums earned" (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31). Each MI uses definitionally consistent language with minor variations in the numerator term:

- **MGIC**: Loss ratio = losses incurred, net ÷ net premiums earned (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Enact**: "Loss ratio is calculated by dividing losses incurred by net earned premiums" (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Essent**: "Loss ratio is calculated by dividing the provision (benefit) for losses and LAE by net premiums earned" (ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **NMI Holdings**: "Loss ratio is calculated by dividing insurance claims and claim expenses by net premiums earned" (NMIH_10-K_2025-12-31).

The denominator (net premiums earned) is consistent across all MIs. The numerator differs slightly by MI — "losses incurred, net" (MGIC, Enact), "provision for losses and LAE" (Essent), or "insurance claims and claim expenses" (NMI) — but all refer to the GAAP loss provision recorded against the MI's income statement, net of reinsurance recoveries.

**Mechanics — why the ratio can be negative.** In MI, loss reserving is fundamentally different from standard P&C. MIs establish case reserves only when a loan becomes delinquent (typically two or more payments past due) and has not cured or resulted in a claim. Reserves are estimated using two key assumptions: the **claim rate** (what percentage of delinquent loans will result in a claim payment) and **claim severity** (the expected dollar amount of that claim). IBNR ("incurred but not reported") reserves are also established for delinquencies estimated to have occurred but not yet reported to the insurer (per MGIC's framework — MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).

Losses incurred each period consist of two components:

1. **Losses on new delinquency notices** received in the current period, which is generally calculated as a function of the claim-rate × claim-severity assumptions applied to the new delinquencies.
2. **Re-estimation of reserves on delinquencies carried over from prior periods** ("prior-year reserve development") — which can be either favorable (reserves released, reducing the loss provision) or adverse (reserves strengthened, increasing the loss provision).

When favorable prior-year reserve development on existing delinquencies *exceeds* losses on new notices for the period, the loss ratio turns negative. This is not an accounting anomaly: it reflects that MIs initially reserve conservatively on new delinquencies and subsequently release reserves as loans cure or claim-rate assumptions improve. Through 2022-2024, the industry recorded multiple years of negative loss ratios driven by post-COVID reserve releases.

**Sensitivity to assumptions.** MGIC notes that a one-percentage-point change in the average claim rate reserve factor would change loss reserves by approximately ±$18 million, and a $1,000 change in average severity would change reserves by ±$7 million (MTG_10-K_2024-12-31). The reserves are highly leveraged to small changes in expected cure performance.

**Segment specifics.** Some MIs report loss ratios at the segment level, which can differ from consolidated figures:
- **MGIC** operates as a single reportable segment, so its 5.1% loss ratio is the consolidated figure (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Essent** reports separately for Mortgage Insurance (16.8% in 2025) and Reinsurance (0.5% in 2025) segments, with a consolidated total loss ratio of 15.7% in 2025 (ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Radian** reports a Mortgage Insurance segment loss ratio of 7.1% in 2025; the company's "All Other" segment was reclassified to discontinued operations in Q3 2025 (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Arch** reports separate loss ratios for Insurance (61.3% in 2025), Reinsurance (56.8% in 2025), and Mortgage ((0.4)% in 2025) segments (ACGL_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Enact** reports a single (mortgage-insurance-focused) loss ratio of 11% for 2025 (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **NMI** reports a single (mortgage-insurance-focused) loss ratio of 9.6% for 2025 (NMIH_10-K_2025-12-31).

## Why it matters

**First, the loss ratio is the primary measure of MI underwriting performance.** Combined with the expense ratio, it produces the combined ratio (the basic measure of underwriting profitability — combined ratio < 100% means underwriting profit). Because of MI's long-tail nature and the asymmetry between when premiums are earned (smoothly over the policy life) and when losses emerge (concentrated in the 3-7 year post-origination window), the loss ratio is also the key signal of where in the credit cycle the in-force book sits.

**Second, loss ratios are highly sensitive to home prices.** Favorable reserve development is heavily driven by home price appreciation, which allows delinquent borrowers to cure through property sales (where the borrower can sell at or above the loan balance) (MTG_10-K_2024-12-31). A sustained downturn in home prices would reverse this dynamic, causing adverse development. The 2022-2024 cycle of negative loss ratios across the industry was supported by the substantial home-price appreciation in the 2020-2022 period, which generated significant embedded equity in delinquent loans.

**Third, vintage-level loss ratios reveal underwriting quality.** Radian discloses a cumulative incurred loss ratio by origination vintage, showing how different underwriting years perform over time. Per Radian's MD&A, the 2025 provision for losses on the Mortgage Insurance segment was $66.8 million (current-year defaults adding $211.4 million to the provision, partially offset by favorable prior-year development of $144.6 million) (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**Fourth, the post-COVID one-time release is winding down.** Through 2022-2024, large favorable reserve releases on COVID-era delinquencies drove loss ratios into deeply negative territory. By 2025, those releases had moderated meaningfully and current-year defaults from more recent vintages had begun to season — pushing every MI's loss ratio into positive territory except Arch's mortgage segment (still slightly negative at (0.4)%). MGIC's 2025 transition is illustrative: $204.1 million of losses on new delinquency notices versus $155.2 million of favorable prior-year development, yielding a positive $48.9 million net loss provision (a 5.1% loss ratio) (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).

## How it has evolved

The loss ratio trajectory across the six MIs over 2022-2025 reflects the unwinding of COVID-era reserving.

**2020-2021 (COVID reserving)**: At the onset of COVID-19, MIs established large case reserves as delinquencies spiked under forbearance programs (with the temporary 0.30x PMIERs multiplier on COVID-era forbearance-related non-performing loans — see [[topics/pmiers]]). Initial assumptions were conservative, anticipating elevated claim rates on the COVID-era delinquencies.

**2022-2024 (reserve releases)**: As home prices surged and forbearance programs facilitated cures (rather than claims), the conservatively-established 2020-2021 reserves proved excessive and were released over multiple years. This produced deeply negative loss ratios at most MIs:
- Radian: (7.3)% in 2022, (4.6)% in 2023 (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).
- Essent (Mortgage Insurance segment, restated): 3.7% in 2023 (ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31). The 2022 figure is not in the 2025 10-K.
- MGIC: losses incurred net was a benefit in both 2023 and 2024 ($(20.9)M and $(14.9)M respectively) (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31 line 565).
- Arch (mortgage segment): (4.4)% in 2024 (ACGL_10-K_2025-12-31).

**2024 (transitional year)**: Most MIs still negative or near-zero — Radian (0.2)%, MGIC (1.5)%, Arch (4.4)%; but several beginning to print positive — NMI 5.6%, Enact 4%, Essent (MI segment) 8.8%.

**2025 (decisive transition into positive territory)**: Every MI's loss ratio moved several points higher in 2025 vs. 2024, marking the broadest year-over-year deterioration since the COVID-era reserve releases began. The dispersion across MIs widened — Arch's mortgage segment is still the only one printing a negative number ((0.4)%), while Essent's MI segment reached 16.8% on $145M of loss provision.

The trajectory reflects two compounding dynamics: (i) **narrowing favorable prior-year reserve development** as the pool of pre-COVID delinquencies shrinks (most have either cured or reached final claim disposition), and (ii) **growing current-year loss content from 2022-2024 vintages** as those origination years season into the 3-4-year post-origination loss emergence window. Per MGIC management at Q4 2025: "the increase in new delinquency development [should] begin to slow, especially as we think about that relative to last year's increase ... that's not a kind of a rule or anything for us. It's really looking at how many are curing, what is the monthly pace and quarterly pace at which they are curing" (MTG_TRANSCRIPT_2025-12-31).

**Forward**: Management commentary in Q4 2025 framed the loss ratio normalization as continuing into 2026, but expects the *pace* of normalization to slow as the post-COVID reserve cushion is increasingly exhausted. Per Radian: "Our cumulative cure rate assumption [is] 92.5% in terms of our reserving assumptions. So we take a fairly conservative view there relative to what we've seen over the last several years" (RDN_TRANSCRIPT_2025-12-31). Per MGIC: "we continue to apply the initial claim rate assumption of 7.5% consistent with recent periods" (MTG_TRANSCRIPT_2025-12-31). The conservatism in initial-reserving assumptions provides scope for continued favorable development, but at a moderating pace.
