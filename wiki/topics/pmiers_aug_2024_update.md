# PMIERs August 2024 Update

> On August 21, 2024, Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and the FHFA released an update to the Private Mortgage Insurer Eligibility Requirements ("PMIERs") that refines the standards for Available Assets — the most-liquid-assets portion of an insurer's balance sheet that is counted toward meeting the Minimum Required Assets capital test. The update differentiates bond credit by quality and liquidity, and establishes new limits on assets backed by residential mortgages or commercial real estate to mitigate the impact of stressed asset value declines. The update is being phased in over a two-year period: 25% of the calculated adjustment was implemented at March 31, 2025; 50% at September 30, 2025; 75% at March 31, 2026; and 100% at September 30, 2026. All six U.S. private mortgage insurers reported in their 2025 10-Ks that the update is not expected to have a material impact on their PMIERs Cushion or capital position once fully implemented (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31; ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31; MTG_10-K_2025-12-31; NMIH_10-K_2025-12-31).

## What it is

The August 21, 2024 PMIERs Update is the most recent material amendment to the GSE-administered PMIERs framework, which establishes when an insurer is eligible to insure loans purchased by Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac. The update focuses on the **Available Assets** side of the PMIERs financial test — i.e., the asset-quality definitions used to determine which assets qualify as "Available" for purposes of meeting Minimum Required Assets — rather than on the Minimum Required Assets calculation itself.

The substantive change has two components (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31):

1. **Bond credit-quality and liquidity differentiation**: Available Asset credit is now refined based on bond credit rating and liquidity, with reductions in the credit granted for certain asset classes. This sharpens the prior framework's treatment of fixed-income securities by pulling credit toward higher-rated, more-liquid bonds and away from lower-rated or less-liquid ones.

2. **Limits on real-estate-backed assets**: The update establishes new limits on Available Assets backed by residential mortgages or commercial real estate. The motivation is to mitigate the risk that those assets — which are positively correlated with the underlying mortgage credit risk MIs insure — could lose value at the same time MI claim activity rises during a housing stress (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

The PMIERs Updates do **not** prohibit a mortgage insurer from holding any type of asset; they limit the **credit** granted under PMIERs for those asset types (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

The phase-in schedule is uniform across all approved insurers and operates as a percentage of the calculated full adjustment, applied at quarter-end:

| Effective Date | % of Full Adjustment Phased In |
|---|---|
| March 31, 2025 | 25% |
| September 30, 2025 | 50% |
| March 31, 2026 | 75% |
| September 30, 2026 | 100% |

Source: ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31.

## Why it matters

Three reasons the update is consequential for the analyst.

First, **it tightens the asset side of the PMIERs ratio without changing the liability side**. PMIERs sufficiency = Available Assets / Minimum Required Assets. Because the update reduces Available Assets credit for certain bond categories and real-estate-backed assets, the same underlying balance sheet produces a lower Available Assets number, all else equal. MIs may respond by reweighting their investment portfolios toward asset classes that retain favorable PMIERs treatment — Enact's 10-K notes the impact "will be influenced by investment portfolio maturities, dispositions, reinvestments, and overall business and economic performance through the phase-in dates" (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

Second, **the impact on each MI is small in dollar terms but visible in the cushion math**. Each MI in its 2025 10-K independently concluded that the update will not have a material impact on its capital position, PMIERs Cushion, or investment portfolio asset allocation strategy:

- **Enact**: "We expect to hold capital sufficiency well in excess of these requirements and do not expect the impact of these updates to be material to our sufficiency" (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Radian**: "The PMIERs Updates have not had and, once fully implemented, are not expected to have a material impact on Radian Guaranty's capital position, its PMIERs Cushion or its investment portfolio asset allocation strategy" (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Essent**: "Essent expects to remain in full compliance with the PMIERs requirements" (ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **MGIC**: MGIC's Available Assets totaled $5.7 billion at December 31, 2025, $2.5 billion in excess of Minimum Required Assets — no material expected impact (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **NMI Holdings**: PMIERs sufficiency was 70% at year-end 2025 (Available Assets $3.5B vs. risk-based required assets $2.1B); the company expects to remain in compliance (NMIH_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Arch**: Continues to meet PMIERs requirements (ACGL_10-K_2025-12-31).

Third, **the update interacts with the discontinuation of the COVID-era 0.30x multiplier (March 31, 2025)** to compress PMIERs cushions modestly during 2025. The 0.30x multiplier had reduced the risk-based required asset factor for non-performing loans related to COVID-era forbearance; its expiry effective March 31, 2025 increased Minimum Required Assets at MIs carrying such loans. At Enact, the multiplier had benefited 2024 required assets by approximately $28 million; combined with the 25% phase-in of the August 2024 update at March 31, 2025 and the 50% phase-in at September 30, 2025, Enact's PMIERs sufficiency ratio declined from 167% at year-end 2024 to 162% at year-end 2025, with the cushion contracting from $2,052M to $1,919M (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

For MIs that have operated with thicker historical cushions (e.g., MGIC at 178% at year-end 2025), the combined effect is small relative to the available capital; for MIs operating closer to the regulatory floor, the phase-in period is a meaningful watch item through September 2026.

## Current state (as of 2025-12-31)

- **Phase-in to date**: 50% phased in (the March 31, 2025 and September 30, 2025 stages) (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31; ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Remaining**: 75% at March 31, 2026 and 100% at September 30, 2026 (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Compliance status**: All six MIs report PMIERs compliance as of December 31, 2025 (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31; ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31; ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31; NMIH_10-K_2025-12-31; ACGL_10-K_2025-12-31).

## How it has evolved

**December 31, 2015**: Original PMIERs effective. Established the Available Assets / Minimum Required Assets financial test framework (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).

**Subsequent amendments through 2024**: Multiple amendments addressed specific matters, including COVID-19 forbearance treatment (the 0.30x multiplier on non-performing loans) and various interim refinements (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**June 30, 2020**: PMIERs amendment introduced the 0.30x multiplier on the risk-based required asset factor for non-performing loans backed by properties in FEMA Declared Major Disaster Areas eligible for individual assistance — extended for COVID-era forbearance non-performing loans (ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**August 21, 2024**: GSEs and FHFA release the PMIERs Update — the focus of this page (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**March 31, 2025**: First phase-in milestone (25% of calculated adjustment); concurrently, the 0.30x multiplier on COVID-era forbearance non-performing loans is discontinued (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**September 30, 2025**: Second phase-in milestone (50%) (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**March 31, 2026**: Third phase-in milestone (75%) (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**September 30, 2026**: Final phase-in milestone (100%) — the update is fully effective (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

The overall trajectory is one of incremental tightening of the asset side of PMIERs, layered on top of the pre-existing risk-based-required-asset side. The GSEs frequently evaluate PMIERs for additional interim changes (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31), so further amendments during the phase-in period are possible.
