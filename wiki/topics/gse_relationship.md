# GSE Relationship

> The U.S. private mortgage insurance industry is structurally co-dependent with Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac (the GSEs). The GSEs' charters generally bar them from purchasing low-down-payment loans (LTV > 80%) without credit enhancement; private mortgage insurance is the most common form of that credit enhancement. The GSEs in turn impose Private Mortgage Insurer Eligibility Requirements ("PMIERs") that the six U.S. mortgage insurers must meet to be approved insurers. As of September 30, 2025, the GSEs held or guaranteed approximately $7.8 trillion of U.S. residential mortgage debt — about 53% of all U.S. 1-4 family residential mortgage debt outstanding (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31). The GSEs have been in FHFA conservatorship since 2008; recent policy developments — January 2025 amendments to the Senior Preferred Stock Purchase Agreements ("PSPAs") and ongoing exploration of recapitalization and a potential limited public offering — keep the future status of the GSEs (and therefore the regulatory floor under the MI industry) actively in flux (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

## What it is

The relationship has three structural pillars.

**The GSE charter requirement.** Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac are the largest participants in the U.S. secondary mortgage market. Their charters generally prohibit purchasing a low-down-payment loan unless one of three forms of credit enhancement is in place: (i) private mortgage insurance from a GSE-approved insurer, (ii) the seller retaining at least a 10% participation in the loan, or (iii) the seller agreeing to repurchase or replace the loan in the event of default. Historically, private mortgage insurance has been the preferred method (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31). Because of this, the U.S. private mortgage insurance industry is, in management's framing, "driven in large part by the business practices and mortgage insurance requirements of the GSEs" (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**PMIERs as the eligibility framework.** Originally adopted effective December 31, 2015, PMIERs is the GSEs' coordinated set of operational and financial requirements that an insurer must meet to be approved to insure loans purchased by the GSEs. The financial component requires that "Available Assets" (the most liquid assets of an insurer, after exclusions and haircuts) meet or exceed "Minimum Required Assets" (a risk-based asset amount calculated from RIF and a battery of risk-factor tables, with reinsurance credits and a $400 million floor) (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31). Operational requirements cover virtually every aspect of an MI's business — underwriting, claims processing, loss mitigation, internal controls, and prior-consent rules for actions like commuting reinsurance or paying capital distributions (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31). PMIERs has been amended multiple times since 2015; the most recent material change is the August 21, 2024 PMIERs Update, phasing in revised Available-Asset standards through September 30, 2026 (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**Conservatorship and the FHFA.** The GSEs have been in FHFA conservatorship since 2008. The U.S. Treasury currently owns the preferred stock of each GSE pursuant to the Senior Preferred Stock Purchase Agreements ("PSPAs"), giving Treasury significant influence over the future status and direction of the GSEs (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). The Director of the FHFA is removable by the President at will, so policy direction is influenced by each Administration (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

## Why it matters

Three points matter most for the analyst.

First, **the GSE relationship is a binary on the asset side**: an MI must remain PMIERs-compliant to write new business on loans the GSEs will acquire. Loss of GSE eligibility would have a material adverse effect on any of the six MIs' business — making PMIERs cushion a closely watched figure (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31). At year-end 2025, the published cushions across the six MIs ranged from MGIC's $2.5 billion (178%) and Essent's $1.4 billion (169%) at the high end, to Enact's $1.9 billion (162%) and Radian's $1.6 billion, to NMIH's $1.4 billion (where NMI's reported "70%" sufficiency ratio is defined differently — see [[pmiers_aug_2024_update]]).

Second, **GSE business practice changes can reshape the size and mix of the MI market**. The PSPAs were amended in January 2021 to restrict the GSEs' acquisition of higher-risk single-family mortgage loans (loans with two or more "higher-risk" characteristics — LTVs > 90%, DTIs > 45%, or FICOs < 680). Those restrictions were suspended in September 2021 and were further amended in January 2025 to eliminate the limitations on higher-risk loans (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). It is uncertain whether or how the current Administration may further amend the PSPAs (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

Third, **the GSEs themselves are a moving target**. Both Trump Administrations have explored, and taken actions directed toward, the potential future release of the GSEs from conservatorship, including (during the first Administration) adopting the Enterprise Regulatory Capital Framework ("ERCF") and allowing the GSEs to retain capital up to ERCF requirements, and (during the current Administration) further efforts to explore recapitalizing the GSEs, including a potential limited public offering of equity interests (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). Any such transition could change MI demand directly (through GSE business practices), or indirectly (through changes to PMIERs, GSE pricing of credit risk, or the role of alternative credit risk-transfer instruments) (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).

There is also a **competitive vector** that the GSE relationship doesn't fully capture: government-supported alternatives. The FHA (Federal Housing Administration) and VA (Department of Veterans Affairs) operate government-backed mortgage insurance programs that compete directly with PMI on low-down-payment loans. In 2025 the FHA's share of the low-down-payment market was approximately 34.3% (vs. 33.5% in 2024) and the VA's share was 26.8% (vs. 24.5%); the PMI industry's share of the *total insured mortgage market* was approximately 38% in 2025, down from 41% in 2024 (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). FHA premium decisions — including the February 2023 30-basis-point reduction in FHA annual premiums and any future cuts — directly affect MI competitive economics (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).

## Current state (as of 2025-12-31)

- **Conservatorship status**: GSEs remain in FHFA conservatorship, as they have been since 2008 (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **PSPA status**: Amended in January 2025 to eliminate prior limitations on the GSEs' acquisition of higher-risk loans; further amendments by the current Administration are uncertain (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **GSE share of U.S. mortgage debt**: Per Federal Reserve data cited in Enact's 10-K, the GSEs held or guaranteed approximately $7.8 trillion as of September 30, 2025 — about 53% of total U.S. 1-4 family residential mortgage debt (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Recapitalization activity**: The current Administration has explored potential GSE recapitalization, including a possible limited public offering of GSE equity interests (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **PMIERs regulatory pipeline**: The August 21, 2024 PMIERs Update is phasing in 25%/50%/75%/100% across March 31, 2025 to September 30, 2026 (see [[pmiers_aug_2024_update]]) (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). The GSEs frequently evaluate PMIERs for additional interim changes (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **Industry NIW vs. government share (2025)**: Total private MI industry NIW was approximately $311 billion in 2025, $299 billion in 2024, and $284 billion in 2023; FHA + VA + USDA combined accounted for approximately 62.0% of the low-down-payment market in 2025 (vs. 58.9% in 2024) (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).
- **GSE credit-risk-transfer programs**: Each GSE operates structured CRT programs (Fannie Mae's CIRT/CAS and Freddie Mac's ACIS/STACR) that transfer mortgage credit risk to private investors and reinsurers — an area in which Essent Re, Arch, and Enact Re are meaningful participants (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31; ACGL_10-K_2025-12-31).

## How it has evolved

**2008**: The GSEs are placed in FHFA conservatorship; Treasury acquires senior preferred stock under the original PSPAs.

**2015**: Original PMIERs effective December 31, 2015 — the GSEs' first coordinated, codified eligibility framework for private mortgage insurers (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31; ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**2019**: Most recent large-scale PMIERs revisions become effective; subsequent amendments through 2024 address specific matters (COVID-19 forbearance treatment, the 0.30x multiplier for FEMA Declared Major Disaster Areas, etc.) (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**2020 (COVID-era)**: GSEs adopt forbearance accommodations and a 0.30x multiplier on the risk-based required asset amount factor for non-performing loans related to COVID-era forbearance — an accommodation that took effect with PMIERs amendments and was discontinued effective March 31, 2025 (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**December 17, 2020**: ERCF (Enterprise Regulatory Capital Framework) promulgated, increasing the amount of capital the GSEs are allowed to retain (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**January 2021**: PSPAs amended to (i) increase the capital the GSEs may retain and (ii) restrict GSE acquisition of higher-risk single-family loans (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**September 2021**: Treasury and FHFA agree to suspend the higher-risk loan acquisition restrictions imposed by the January 2021 PSPA amendments (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**February 2023**: FHA reduces its annual mortgage insurance premium by 30 basis points for most new borrowers — a competitive event that affected PMI market share dynamics (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**August 21, 2024**: GSEs and FHFA release the PMIERs Update, refining the standards for Available Assets (differentiating bonds by credit quality and liquidity, and limiting credit for assets backed by residential mortgages or commercial real estate). Phase-in: 25% at March 31, 2025; 50% at September 30, 2025; 75% at March 31, 2026; 100% at September 30, 2026 (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**January 2025**: PSPAs further amended to eliminate the higher-risk loan acquisition limitations (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**March 31, 2025**: Use of the 0.30x PMIERs multiplier for COVID-related non-performing loans is discontinued (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31).

**Late 2025 / 2026 (in process)**: Further exploration by the current Administration of GSE recapitalization, including a potential limited public offering of GSE equity interests (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).
