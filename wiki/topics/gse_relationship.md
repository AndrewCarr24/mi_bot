# GSE Relationship

> The U.S. private mortgage insurance industry is structurally co-dependent with Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac (the "Enterprises" or "GSEs"). The Enterprises are statutorily required to obtain credit enhancement on any single-family loan they purchase with an LTV greater than 80%; private mortgage insurance is, per Freddie Mac's PMI Handbook, "the most commonly used form of credit enhancement" (INDUSTRY_FREDDIE_PMI_HANDBOOK_2021-09). The GSEs in turn impose Private Mortgage Insurer Eligibility Requirements ("PMIERs") that an MI must satisfy to be an "approved insurer" (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA; INDUSTRY_PMIERS_2.0_BASE; see [[topics/pmiers]]). The GSEs have been in FHFA conservatorship since September 6, 2008 — the longest conservatorship of any financial institution in American history — and the Senior Preferred Stock Purchase Agreements ("PSPAs") between Treasury and each Enterprise have been amended multiple times since 2008 (three formal amendments in 2009-2012 plus letter agreements in 2017, 2019, 2021, and 2025) to evolve the capital, dividend, and acquisition rules (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). At year-end 2024, the Enterprises owned or guaranteed approximately $7.6 trillion of U.S. single-family and multifamily mortgages — nearly half of the market — with combined net worth of $154.3 billion ($94.7 billion at Fannie Mae, $59.6 billion at Freddie Mac), in both cases still significantly less than required under the post-conservatorship Enterprise Regulatory Capital Framework ("ERCF") (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024; INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024). Recent policy moves — January 2025 PSPA amendments eliminating prior limits on higher-risk loan acquisition (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31), March-April 2025 board-of-directors changes at both Enterprises with FHFA Director William J. Pulte serving as Chair of each board (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024), and exploration of a potential limited public offering of GSE equity (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31) — keep the future status of the GSEs (and therefore the regulatory framework underneath the MI industry) actively in flux.

## What it is

The relationship between the GSEs and the U.S. private mortgage insurance industry has three structural pillars.

**The GSE charter requirement.** Fannie Mae was established in 1938 and Freddie Mac in 1970 to provide stability and liquidity in the secondary market for residential mortgages (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024). Each Enterprise's charter act requires that any loan it purchases with an outstanding principal balance exceeding 80 percent of the property value have an acceptable form of credit enhancement (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA, citing 12 U.S.C. 1454(a) and 12 U.S.C. 1717(b); INDUSTRY_FREDDIE_PMI_HANDBOOK_2021-09). The three forms of charter-acceptable credit enhancement are (i) PMI from a GSE-approved insurer, (ii) the seller retaining at least a 10% participation in the loan, and (iii) the seller agreeing to repurchase or replace the loan in the event of default (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31). PMI is consistently the preferred method (INDUSTRY_FREDDIE_PMI_HANDBOOK_2021-09; INDUSTRY_USMI_RESILIENCY_2023-11).

The Enterprises specify their MI coverage requirements by LTV range. Per Freddie Mac's PMI Handbook, the standard coverage requirement on a fixed-rate mortgage with 90% LTV is **25% MI coverage**, meaning that in the event of a claim the MI is responsible for paying 25% of the outstanding balance plus claimable expenses, reducing the lender or investor's ultimate loss exposure (INDUSTRY_FREDDIE_PMI_HANDBOOK_2021-09). The standard coverage table is:

| LTV ratio | Fixed Rate Term ≤ 20 years | Fixed Rate Term > 20 years, ARMs, Manufactured Housing |
|---|---|---|
| 80% < LTV ≤ 85% | 6% | 12% |
| 85% < LTV ≤ 90% | 12% | 25% |
| 90% < LTV ≤ 95% | 25% | 30% |
| 95% < LTV ≤ 97%* | — | 35% |

*Home Possible Mortgages with LTV > 95% are capped at 25% MI coverage. Source: INDUSTRY_FREDDIE_PMI_HANDBOOK_2021-09.

The Freddie Mac PMI Handbook also defines the three claim payment options for the MI: the **Percentage Option** (Claim Amount × MI Coverage Percentage; most common), the **Third-Party Sale Option** (lesser of Claim Amount minus net proceeds or the Percentage Option), and the **Acquisition Option** (100% × Claim Amount, with the MI taking title to the property in settlement) (INDUSTRY_FREDDIE_PMI_HANDBOOK_2021-09).

**PMIERs as the eligibility framework.** The Enterprises operate at the direction of FHFA, which is their Conservator. Each Enterprise's charter act allows the Enterprise to determine whether a mortgage insurer is qualified to insure loans the Enterprise acquires (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA). The PMIERs framework, finalized in 2015 and updated through PMIERs 2.0 in 2018 and Guidance documents in 2024, is the codified set of operational and financial standards that an MI must meet to be a Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae approved insurer (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA; INDUSTRY_PMIERS_2.0_BASE). For full detail, see [[topics/pmiers]].

**Conservatorship and the FHFA.** FHFA was established by the Housing and Economic Recovery Act of 2008 ("HERA") and placed Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac into conservatorship on September 6, 2008 to "preserve and conserve the Enterprises' assets and property while restoring the Enterprises to a sound and solvent condition," allowing them to continue fulfilling their statutory missions (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024; INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). FHFA, as Conservator, succeeded to all rights, titles, powers, and privileges of the Enterprises, including stockholder rights, board rights, and management authority (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). The conservatorships are the longest in American financial history (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**The PSPAs.** Concurrent with the conservatorship, each Enterprise entered into a Senior Preferred Stock Purchase Agreement ("PSPA") with the U.S. Department of the Treasury on September 7, 2008, amended and restated on September 26, 2008 (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024). The PSPAs commit Treasury to providing funding for each Enterprise (up to a maximum available funding commitment) to ensure each maintains a non-negative net worth — preventing the statutory requirement that an Enterprise be put into receivership (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024). The PSPAs originally required each Enterprise to pay a 10% dividend to Treasury; subsequent amendments (May 6, 2009; December 24, 2009; August 17, 2012) included changing the dividend structure to a "net worth sweep" that required the Enterprises to remit all earnings above a minimum capital reserve to Treasury (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024).

**Enterprise Regulatory Capital Framework ("ERCF").** Promulgated December 17, 2020 (with subsequent amendments through 2023), the ERCF is a post-conservatorship regulatory capital standard requiring each Enterprise to hold (i) 4.5% common equity tier 1 capital, (ii) 6% tier 1 capital, (iii) 8% adjusted total capital (all based on risk-weighted assets), plus an additional risk-based capital buffer; and a leverage requirement of 2.5% tier 1 capital ratio plus a leverage buffer (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). At year-end 2024, neither Enterprise met its minimum regulatory capital requirements under the ERCF (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**FHFA Director appointment.** The FHFA Director is removable by the President at will, so policy direction is influenced by each Administration. William J. Pulte assumed the position in 2025 (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

## Why it matters

Three reasons the GSE relationship is the central regulatory dimension for U.S. private mortgage insurers.

First, **the GSE relationship is binary on the asset side**. An MI must remain PMIERs-compliant to write new business on loans the GSEs will acquire; loss of GSE eligibility "would have a material adverse effect" on the MI's business (ACT_10-K_2025-12-31; ESNT_10-K_2025-12-31). Substantially all of the six MIs' new insurance written is on loans purchased by the GSEs (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31). At year-end 2025, all six U.S. MIs reported PMIERs compliance, with cushions ranging from approximately $1.4 billion (Essent, NMI, Radian) to $2.5 billion (MGIC); see [[topics/pmiers]].

Second, **GSE business-practice changes can reshape the MI market**. The Enterprises' acquisition rules — what loans they will buy, at what guarantee fee, with what MI coverage — directly shape PMI demand. The PSPAs were amended in January 2021 to (i) raise the cap on retained-earnings buffer to amounts necessary to meet ERCF capital requirements (effectively ending the prior net worth sweep) and (ii) restrict GSE acquisition of higher-risk single-family loans (those with two or more "higher-risk" characteristics — LTVs > 90%, DTIs > 45%, or FICOs < 680) and impose limits on cash window volumes, multifamily lending, second homes, and investor properties (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). Those acquisition restrictions were suspended in September 2021 (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31; INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024) and were further amended in January 2025 to eliminate the limitations on higher-risk loans (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). It is uncertain whether or how the current Administration may further amend the PSPAs, including whether it will reinstate prior limitations or impose new ones (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

Third, **the GSEs themselves are a moving target**. Both Trump Administrations have taken actions directed toward the potential future release of the GSEs from conservatorship (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). During the first Trump Administration, those actions included adopting the ERCF and allowing the GSEs to retain capital up to ERCF requirements (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). The current Administration has continued exploring recapitalization, including a potential limited public offering of equity interests in the GSEs (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). In March-April 2025 the boards of directors of both Enterprises underwent extensive changes — eight directors were removed from the Fannie Mae board and six from the Freddie Mac board, with FHFA Director William J. Pulte appointed as Chair of each board (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). Any release-from-conservatorship transition could change MI demand directly (through GSE business practices), or indirectly (through changes to PMIERs, GSE pricing of credit risk, or the use of alternative credit-risk-transfer instruments) (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31).

## Current state (as of 2025-12-31)

**Conservatorship status**: GSEs remain in FHFA conservatorship, as they have been since September 6, 2008 (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024).

**FHFA Director**: William J. Pulte, who assumed the position in 2025 (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**Enterprise net worth and capital**: At year-end 2024, Fannie Mae's net worth was $94.7 billion (a 21.9% increase year-over-year) and Freddie Mac's was $59.6 billion (a 24.8% increase year-over-year). Neither met the ERCF minimum capital requirements (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). Fannie Mae reported 2024 net income of $17.0 billion (vs. $17.4 billion in 2023); Freddie Mac reported 2024 net income of $11.9 billion (vs. $10.5 billion in 2023) (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**Combined Enterprise guarantee book**: As of December 31, 2024, the Enterprises owned or guaranteed approximately $7.6 trillion in single-family and multifamily mortgages, representing nearly half of the market (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). At Fannie Mae, single-family was 88.4% of the total guarantee book and multifamily was 11.6%, with the year-end multifamily portfolio at $502.6 billion. Combined 2024 acquisition volumes: $672 billion single-family (vs. $616 billion in 2023) and $120 billion multifamily (vs. $101 billion in 2023) (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**Enterprise CRT activity**: In 2024 the Enterprises transferred credit risk on single-family mortgages with approximately $365 billion of UPB and $11.9 billion of RIF — the second consecutive year of reduced CRT volume, driven by lower single-family acquisition volume. Cumulatively since the programs began in 2013, the Enterprises have transferred risk on loans with approximately $7 trillion of UPB and $222 billion of RIF (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**PSPA status**: Last amended in January 2025 to eliminate the limitations on higher-risk loan acquisitions added in January 2021 (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). Further amendments by the current Administration are uncertain. The amended PSPAs allow each Enterprise to retain all its earnings (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). Under the PSPAs, while in conservatorship, the Enterprises are constrained in their ability to take certain actions without prior written consent of Treasury (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024).

**ERCF status**: Both Enterprises remain materially below the ERCF capital requirements. As of December 31, 2024, total equity at the Enterprises ($94.7 billion at Fannie, $59.6 billion at Freddie) was significantly less than required capital under the framework (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**Recent governance changes**: In March 2025, eight directors were removed from the Fannie Mae Board of Directors (Amy Alving, Christopher Brummer, Michael Heid, Simon Johnson, Diane Lye, Diane Nordin, Chetlur Ragavan, Michael Seelig). Michael Stucky, FHFA General Counsel Clinton Jones, and FHFA Director William J. Pulte were appointed, with Director Pulte serving as Chair (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024). Also in March, six directors were removed from the Freddie Mac Board (Kevin Chavers, Lance Drummond, Luke Hayden, Allan Merrill, Jane Prokop, Ray Swan); Brandon Hamara, Clinton Jones, Ralph "Cody" Kittle, and Director Pulte were appointed, with Director Pulte serving as Chair. Mike Hutchins was named President and Interim CEO of Freddie Mac following the termination of former CEO Diana Reid in March 2025 (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**PMI demand in the GSE-acquisition channel**: Substantially all of the six MIs' NIW is on GSE-acquired loans (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31; ACT_10-K_2025-12-31). 2025 PMI industry NIW was approximately $311 billion per *Inside Mortgage Finance* (MTG_10-K_2025-12-31), versus 2024 industry NIW of $299 billion and 2023 industry NIW of $284 billion. See [[topics/us_mortgage_market]].

## How it has evolved

**1938 / 1970**: Fannie Mae (1938) and Freddie Mac (1970) chartered by Congress to provide stability and liquidity in the secondary market for residential mortgages (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024).

**2003 / 2008**: Original Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac mortgage insurer eligibility requirements published separately. Each Enterprise relied primarily on rating-agency ratings rather than specific counterparty risk and financial standards (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA).

**September 6, 2008**: FHFA appointed Conservator of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac under the Federal Housing Enterprises Financial Safety and Soundness Act of 1992, as amended by HERA (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024). The conservatorships remain in place at year-end 2025, the longest in American financial history (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**September 7, 2008 / September 26, 2008**: Original PSPAs executed and then amended/restated. The PSPAs commit Treasury to provide funding (up to a maximum) to ensure each Enterprise maintains a non-negative net worth (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024).

**May 6, 2009 / December 24, 2009 / August 17, 2012**: First, second, and third PSPA amendments. The third (August 2012) amendment changed the dividend from a fixed 10% quarterly to a variable amount tied to quarterly performance — equivalent to positive net worth above a minimum capital reserve, the "net worth sweep" (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024).

**December 31, 2015**: Original PMIERs effective — first GSE-coordinated codified eligibility framework for private mortgage insurers (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_OVERVIEW_FHFA; see [[topics/pmiers]]).

**December 2017**: Letter agreement permitting each Enterprise to retain a $3 billion capital reserve (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024).

**September 27, 2018**: PMIERs 2.0 base text issued. Effective March 31, 2019 for existing approved insurers (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_2.0_BASE).

**September 27, 2019**: Letter amendment increasing the cap on the capital reserve to $25 billion for Fannie Mae and $20 billion for Freddie Mac, conditionally eliminating the Enterprises' quarterly dividend obligation (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024).

**December 17, 2020**: ERCF promulgated, establishing post-conservatorship regulatory capital framework (4.5% CET1 / 6% Tier 1 / 8% adjusted total capital under risk-weighted requirements; 2.5% tier 1 leverage capital ratio; plus buffers) (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**January 14, 2021**: PSPA amendments effectively ending the net worth sweep — increased the retained-earnings amount each Enterprise may keep to the level necessary to meet ERCF capital requirements and buffers. Also: (i) restricted the GSEs' acquisition of higher-risk single-family loans (LTV > 90% + DTI > 45% + FICO < 680), (ii) imposed limits on cash windows, multifamily lending, second homes and investor property loans (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**September 2021**: Treasury and FHFA agree to suspend certain provisions added in January 2021 — including the higher-risk loan acquisition restrictions, the cash-window limits, the multifamily-lending limits, and the second-home / investor-property limits (INDUSTRY_FHFA_PAR_2024; RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**February 2023**: FHA reduces its annual mortgage insurance premium by 30 basis points for most new borrowers — competitive event affecting PMI economics (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**August 21, 2024**: GSEs and FHFA release PMIERs Guidance 2024-01, refining Available Asset standards (see [[topics/pmiers]]). PMIERs Guidance 2024-02 follows, discontinuing the COVID-era extension of the 0.30x multiplier on non-performing loans effective March 31, 2025 (INDUSTRY_PMIERS_GUIDANCE_2024-01; INDUSTRY_PMIERS_GUIDANCE_2024-02).

**January 2025**: PSPAs further amended to eliminate the limitations on higher-risk loan acquisitions that were imposed in January 2021 (and suspended in September 2021) (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). It is uncertain whether or how the current Administration may further amend the PSPAs (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31).

**March 2025**: Extensive changes to the boards of directors at both Enterprises. Eight directors removed from Fannie Mae board, six from Freddie Mac board; FHFA Director William J. Pulte appointed Chair of each. Mike Hutchins named President and Interim CEO of Freddie Mac following termination of former CEO Diana Reid in March 2025 (INDUSTRY_FHFA_ANNUAL_REPORT_2024).

**Forward**: Current Administration continues exploring recapitalizing the GSEs, including a potential limited public offering of equity interests (RDN_10-K_2025-12-31). Any such transition could alter MI demand or PMIERs.
